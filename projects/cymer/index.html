Cymer DVT

A design verification testing system written in Python for the timing and energy module for Cymer's EUV light source.


Work Project
Details and code may be limited

Disclaimer:

To remain in accordance with my NDA, this write up will contain only publicly available knowledge from sources including but not limited to Cymer/ASML's public-facing webpages, publicly available patent applications, or optics journals.

Concept:

Cymer is a San Diego based company that produces deep ultraviolet and extreme ultraviolet (EUV) light sources used for semiconductor lithography. EUV is significant because it provides the means to advance semiconductor feature size below the 22nm node. The work that I did at Cymer was the production of an automated design verification system for the Timing and Energy Module for their EUV system.

EUV and Plasma-Produced-Lasers:

Cymer's EUV systems produce 13.5nm laser light through a method known as plasma-produced-lasers. A high energy laser, generated in the sub-fab is transmitted up to a vacuum chamber on the fab level. The vacuum chamber is required because at 13.5nm, the light is absorbed quickly in air. The laser is then focused onto a spot in front of a large concave focusing mirror. Tin droplets of about 30um in diameter are fired in front of the mirror where they are hit by the high energy laser at a rate of 50kHz. The tin is vaporized into laser-produced-plasma which generates photons at approximately 13.5nm. These photos are collected and reflected by the mirror where they are transmitted to a focus point and enter the scanner. In the scanner, the EUV light is projected onto a semiconductor mask where it produces the required features on a silicon wafer.

Pre-pulse and Main-Pulse:

In order to ensure that a maximum amount of plasma is produced by the tin droplet, the droplet is illuminated twice by the 30kW; these illuminations are known as pre-pulse and main-pulse. Pre-pulse hits the droplet at a lower energy than main-pulse and flattens the droplet into a pancake shape; this allows a larger surface area of the droplet to be exposed to the main laser pulse.

Timing and Energy Module:

The Timing and Energy module (TEM) is a system within the high energy laser system which monitors signals from the high energy laser system generators as well as sets triggers for the pre-pulse and main-pulse triggers from a primary pulse trigger or to meet certain specifications. The actions of the TEM are controlled by an internal FPGA and the module includes a variety of interfaces.

Testing the TEM:

The engineers at Cymer wanted a Design Verification Test (DVT) to ensure that changes made to the TEM's code did not affect the overall functionality before the code in the clean room module was updated. This would simplify the debug process because it would reduce the number of times that engineers would need to suit up to resolve problems on clean room equipment.

An existing set of test scripts was written for a previous TEM version, however significant changes to the TEM prevented this old script from working properly. The old testing system took approximately 8 hours to run and required continuous operator presence to adjust the test conditions. I wrote a new suite of Python scripts to test the inputs, outputs, and functionality of the TEM system. The suite utilized an RS232 serial connection to write data to registers in the FPGA in the TEM; this method provided a means to control the functionality and output characteristics of the TEM. The outputs of the TEM were routed through a four port output mux to an oscilloscope. The oscilloscope was connected to the script PC via ethernet. This oscilloscope connection was used to determine if the outputs of the TEM were functioning correctly. The scripts had a variety of modes: 'no operator' which runs only tests with no operator intervention required, 'test select' which allows user selection of which tests to run, 'full output' which used the actual output ports instead of the front output mux. Additional scripts were developed for manual and automated reading and writing of FPGA registers.

Result:

The Python scripts resulted in a suite of over 8000 tests on a variety of outputs with 6 out of 9 of the test procedures requiring no operator intervention. Including scripts that require operator intervention, the testing process took 1 hour, a 7 hour reduction in operator-on-bench time from the previous script.
